Thoughts on "Lessons of the War for Science (1944-5), Chris Ryan

"Lessons of the War for Science", J. D. Bernal, Proceedings of the Royal Society of London. Series A, Mathematical and Physical Sciences, Apr. 15, 1975, Vol. 342, No. 1631, pp. 555-574. Originally published in Reports in Progress in Physics in 1944-1945. [link]

My personal exploration:

J.D. Bernal is an important figure in the history of science. He was himself a celebrated molecular biologist, but also wrote extensively about the history of science (see the four-volume epic "Science in History" published in 1954), and played a significant role in popularizing science in the post-World War II world. He was also an outspoken communist and socialist utopian. I may touch on the latter point in my later writings.

The article I am reviewing here was written by Bernal in 1945, shortly after the end of the war. Bernal is writing to an audience of scientists, assessing this communities contributions to the war. Here he seems to be largley taking on his role as a popularizer, encouraging scientists to take up causes more of interest to the public than the halls of academia.

In reviewing the contributions of science to the war effort, Bernal acknowledges, but does not dwell on, the greatest hits of World War II science: the atomic bomb, rocket technology, radar, synthetic chemistry. Instead he writes:

"... the direct contribution of war science to peace science is only a very small part, in any case, of the valuable lessons of the war. Much more important is the new attitude towards science that was engendered in the war and the new integration that grew up between science and practical human activity." [page 555] What is this "new attitude towards science"? It's that science can and should be used towards achieving practical goals. As discussed in Steven Shapin's book ``The Scientific Life'', there is a long tradition of disinterestedness among ``pure'' scientists, their work is that is discovery and curiosity, not profit or the seeking of advantage. A scientist might become involved in commerce or government, but this spoke to some taint in their purity. One of the consequences of World War II was that scientific involvement with the affairs of the state become perceived as essential but also moral and just. Bernal then goes on to detail that "operational research" (OR) as a prime illustration on the success of this "new attitude towards science" and was a development maybe even more crucial to peace time than some of the more ``direct'' applications of science. He wirtes: "The war ... introduced what is virtually an entirely new technique in the application of science, operational research. Though having some affinities with certain commercial inquiries such as market research, it arose independently in the war out of immediate ends and developed so rapidly and so effectively that by the end of the war it was considered a necessary adjunct to every aspect of military affairs." [page 562] - scientists developed new technologies, but military people were reluctant to use them - so scientists interceded, educated, showed them how it would be useful and could actually work - the "operations" of the world is worthy of being studied by scientists and warned against over-specialization - "it was found that scientists were very much more interchangeable than their specialized training would have led us to expect, and that, particularly in new branches of scientific war work such as operational research, scientists trained in all subjects could make equally significant contribution. ... War experience has already taught us the need to keep our scientific manpower flexible by avoiding over-specialization and inculcating general scientific method into teaching." = 561 - CONTRAST: A scientist can work on BASIC or FUNDAMENTAL RESEARCH, but it could also look at OPERATIONAL RESEARCH: how theory bears out in practice. Bernal emphasizes that the practical side involves many human factors: "... [in] the analysis of performance of a particular tactic, such as air submarine-chasing or the bombing of targets; all the factors, and not only the instrumental factors, had to be brought in. Such factors involved many human elements: the morale or fatigue of the soldier or airman, their state of training, the reactions of the enemy .... It was found ... that biologists and social scientists had a flair for operational research equal to if not greater than that of physicists or chemists." - 563 "Operational research, dealing as it did with actual achievement, provided a quantitative corrective to theorizing. It also enabled the scientist to appreciate the human qualities of command and performance and the conditions under which it is necessary to make and adhere to decisions where many of the vital factors cannot possibly be determined in time or at all." - 564 "The knowledge accumulated in operational research and, perhaps even more, the experience of those who took part in it, may prove one of the most valuable contributions of the war to science .... We have found here a necessary link between laboratory science and industry." - 565 "the social scientists who have to find out what people want and need, and the physical scientists who hope to find the ways to satisfy those needs, will need to work together in the closest collaboration." - 565 " One of the great contributions of the war to industry was the introduction of statistical quality control, and with it the possibility of far better quantitative planning of production processes" - page 566 - actually this occured earlier "The function of a scientist as a scientist does not extend into this field. Responsibility for making decisions which are basically indeterminate and must take unassessable factors of human attitudes into account is the proper field of executive authority .... There was a saying in the services that the scientist must be 'on tap' but not 'on top'!" - page 567
